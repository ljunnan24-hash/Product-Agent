# SignalShelf

SignalShelf is a local-first research notebook for tiny teams that launch products on GitHub, Product Hunt, X, Reddit, and Discord. It turns scattered launch signals into a weekly "what changed and what to try next" memo.

## Target users

- Indie hackers who ship one product at a time and do not have a growth analyst.
- Open-source maintainers who want to notice adoption signals before they become obvious.
- Small agencies that run launch experiments for technical products.

## Problem

Teams collect weak signals in too many places: GitHub stars, issues, Discord comments, waitlist notes, Product Hunt comments, newsletter replies, and analytics screenshots. Most of these signals are reviewed manually, so the team either overreacts to vanity metrics or misses repeated objections.

## Current product

SignalShelf imports public links and local notes, extracts launch signals, groups them into themes, and produces a weekly decision memo:

- Strongest user segment this week.
- Top repeated objection.
- Evidence that supports a pricing or positioning change.
- Three next experiments with success criteria.

The MVP is a local desktop/web app. Users can upload CSV exports, paste public links, and write notes. The app stores everything locally by default.

## Early traction

- 41 waitlist signups from one founder newsletter mention.
- 12 user interviews completed.
- 7 of 12 interviews mentioned "I do not know which feedback matters."
- 5 users asked for GitHub issue and Discord import.
- 3 users said they would pay if the tool saved at least one weekly review meeting.

## Risks

- The product may be perceived as a lightweight notes app rather than a decision tool.
- Many target users already use Notion, Airtable, Linear, or spreadsheets.
- Automated summaries can create false confidence if evidence quality is weak.
- A local-first setup is good for trust, but it may reduce collaboration value.

## Current questions

1. Is the "weekly decision memo" strong enough as the wedge?
2. Should the first paid user be indie hackers, open-source maintainers, or agencies?
3. What proof should we collect before building team collaboration?
4. Are GitHub and Discord integrations must-have for the first public beta?
